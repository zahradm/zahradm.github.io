My Journey into Natural Language Processing

February 23, 2026

Introduction Link to heading

Natural Language Processing (NLP) has been one of the most exciting fields in artificial intelligence over the past decade. As a software engineer with a background in statistics, my path into NLP was both challenging and rewarding. In this post, I want to share how I got started, the key concepts that shaped my understanding, and some practical advice for anyone looking to dive into this field.

How It All Started Link to heading

During my bachelor’s degree in statistics at Alzahra University, I became fascinated by the intersection of mathematics and language. I realized that the statistical methods I was learning could be applied to understand and generate human language. This curiosity led me to pursue a master’s degree in software engineering at IUST, where I could combine my statistical background with computer science.

Key Concepts in NLP Link to heading

Here are some fundamental concepts that anyone starting in NLP should be familiar with:

  • Tokenization – Breaking text into meaningful units (words, subwords, or characters)
  • Word Embeddings – Representing words as dense vectors in a continuous space (e.g., Word2Vec, GloVe)
  • Transformer Architecture – The backbone of modern NLP models like BERT, GPT, and their variants
  • Fine-tuning – Adapting pre-trained models to specific downstream tasks
  • Robustness – Ensuring models perform reliably under adversarial or out-of-distribution inputs

Why Robustness Matters Link to heading

My main research interest is NLP models’ robustness. In real-world applications, models often encounter inputs that differ significantly from their training data. A model that achieves high accuracy on benchmark datasets but fails on slightly perturbed inputs is not truly reliable.

Some common challenges include:

  1. Adversarial attacks – Small, intentional perturbations designed to fool models
  2. Domain shift – Performance degradation when applied to a different domain than the training data
  3. Noisy inputs – Typos, slang, and informal language that models may not handle well

Practical Tips for Getting Started Link to heading

If you’re interested in getting started with NLP, here are my recommendations:

  1. Start with the basics – Understand tokenization, bag-of-words models, and TF-IDF before jumping to transformers
  2. Learn Python and key libraries – Get comfortable with transformers (Hugging Face), spaCy, and NLTK
  3. Read research papers – Start with seminal papers like “Attention Is All You Need” and “BERT: Pre-training of Deep Bidirectional Transformers”
  4. Build projects – Apply what you learn to real datasets. Kaggle competitions are a great starting point
  5. Join the community – Follow NLP researchers on social media, attend conferences, and contribute to open-source projects

Conclusion Link to heading

NLP is a rapidly evolving field with incredible potential. Whether you come from a statistics, computer science, or linguistics background, there is a place for you. The key is to stay curious, keep learning, and never stop building.
